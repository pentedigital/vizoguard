Secure VPN: What Makes a VPN Truly Secure?

Not all VPNs are created equal. The word "secure" appears on the marketing page of virtually every VPN product on the market — but the technical reality behind that claim varies enormously. A VPN that uses outdated encryption, routes DNS queries outside its tunnel, or lacks a kill switch is not a secure VPN, regardless of what the product page says.

This guide cuts through the marketing language. We examine the specific technical attributes that define a genuinely secure VPN: encryption standards, protocols, operational security features like kill switches and DNS leak protection, and the logging policies that determine whether a provider can expose your data even when compelled to do so. If you want to understand what you are actually buying when you choose a VPN, start here.

Quick Summary

A truly secure VPN requires AES-256 or ChaCha20 encryption, a modern protocol (WireGuard, OpenVPN, or Shadowsocks for censorship resistance), a verified zero-log policy, a network-level kill switch, and DNS leak prevention. Security claims without independent verification are marketing — not guarantees. This guide explains how to evaluate each component and what to look for beyond the feature list.

What Does "Secure VPN" Actually Mean?

Security in a VPN context operates across multiple layers simultaneously. When someone says a VPN is "secure," they could mean any combination of the following:

  • Encryption security: The cipher and key length used to encrypt data in transit are computationally unbreakable with current technology.
  • Protocol security: The VPN protocol (WireGuard, OpenVPN, IKEv2, etc.) has been formally audited and is free of known cryptographic weaknesses.
  • Operational security: The VPN correctly implements features like a kill switch, DNS leak prevention, and split tunneling to prevent accidental data exposure.
  • Policy security: The provider's logging policy means they hold no data that could be exposed by a breach, subpoena, or legal order.
  • Threat security: The VPN actively detects and blocks malicious traffic — phishing domains, malware connections, surveillance — rather than merely encrypting it.

Most consumer VPN services address the first two layers adequately. The differentiation happens at layers three through five. A VPN that encrypts your traffic perfectly but still leaks your DNS queries to your ISP has a fundamental security gap. A VPN with a claimed no-log policy that has never been independently audited or tested provides no verifiable assurance. Understanding all five layers is how you evaluate whether a VPN is genuinely secure — or just marketed as one.

Encryption Standards: AES-256 and ChaCha20

Encryption is the mathematical process that converts your readable traffic into ciphertext that only your VPN server can decrypt. The strength of that encryption depends on the algorithm (cipher) and key length used.

AES-256: The Government Standard

AES-256 (Advanced Encryption Standard, 256-bit key) is the most widely used cipher in secure VPNs and the standard adopted by the US government for top-secret data classification. The "256" refers to the key length: with a 256-bit key, there are 2256 possible keys — a number so large that brute-forcing the key would take longer than the current age of the universe, even with all computing power on Earth running simultaneously. AES-256 has never been publicly broken. Any VPN claiming to be secure should be using at minimum AES-128; AES-256 is strongly preferred for sensitive use.

AES-256 typically operates in GCM (Galois/Counter Mode), which provides both encryption and authentication in a single pass — meaning the receiver can verify that the data has not been tampered with in transit. This is important: encryption without authentication allows an attacker to flip bits in ciphertext and alter your traffic without being detected.

ChaCha20-Poly1305: The Mobile Alternative

ChaCha20 is a stream cipher developed by Daniel Bernstein, the cryptographer behind many of the algorithms used in WireGuard. ChaCha20-Poly1305 (the cipher paired with the Poly1305 message authentication code) is now a first-class standard in TLS 1.3 and used natively in WireGuard.

ChaCha20 offers two practical advantages over AES-256:

  • Performance on ARM processors: Most mobile devices use ARM chips, which often lack hardware acceleration for AES (AES-NI). ChaCha20 is a pure software implementation that runs significantly faster on these devices without sacrificing security.
  • Resistance to timing attacks: ChaCha20 is inherently constant-time, which eliminates a class of side-channel attacks that can theoretically affect AES implementations that do not take special care.

ChaCha20-Poly1305 is considered equivalent to AES-256-GCM in security strength. If your VPN uses WireGuard, your traffic is encrypted with ChaCha20-Poly1305 by default. Both are excellent choices — the important thing is that your VPN specifies which cipher it uses and does not fall back to weaker alternatives like AES-128-CBC or, worse, PPTP's RC4 (which is thoroughly broken).

Perfect Forward Secrecy

Beyond the cipher, a secure VPN implements Perfect Forward Secrecy (PFS). PFS generates a unique session key for each connection using an ephemeral Diffie-Hellman key exchange. Even if an attacker recorded all your VPN traffic and later compromised your long-term private key, they could not decrypt past sessions — each session's key existed only for that session and was never stored. PFS is standard in WireGuard and OpenVPN when correctly configured; look for explicit mention of it in a provider's security documentation.

VPN Protocols Compared: WireGuard, OpenVPN, Shadowsocks, IKEv2

The encryption cipher determines how data is scrambled. The protocol determines how that encryption is applied, how authentication works, how connections are established, and how the VPN traffic looks to an outside observer. Protocol choice directly affects both security and censorship resistance.

WireGuard

WireGuard is the current state of the art for VPN protocols. Developed by Jason Donenfeld and first released in 2015, it became part of the Linux kernel in 2020 and has since been adopted by NordVPN, Mullvad, ProtonVPN, and many others. Its defining feature is simplicity: roughly 4,000 lines of code versus OpenVPN's 100,000+. Fewer lines means a smaller attack surface, easier auditing, and fewer places for bugs to hide. WireGuard uses only modern, well-vetted cryptographic primitives (ChaCha20, Poly1305, Curve25519 for key exchange, BLAKE2s for hashing, SipHash for hashtable keys) and has been formally verified.

WireGuard's principal limitation is that it assigns static IP addresses to VPN peers, which could in theory allow a VPN provider to correlate connections to users. Providers that care about this (Mullvad, ProtonVPN) implement IP randomization at the application layer. For most users, this is not a practical concern.

OpenVPN

OpenVPN has been the de facto standard VPN protocol for over two decades. It is open source, extensively audited, and supports a wide range of ciphers and configuration options. OpenVPN runs over either UDP (faster) or TCP (more reliable on restrictive networks) and can be configured to run on port 443 — the HTTPS port — making it difficult for network administrators to block without disrupting normal web traffic.

OpenVPN's age and configurability are both strengths and weaknesses. A correctly configured OpenVPN deployment is highly secure. But the flexibility means misconfigured setups are common, and the protocol's identifiable signature means it is susceptible to deep packet inspection (DPI) on heavily censored networks. For users in countries with extensive censorship, OpenVPN on port 443 is better than most alternatives — but Shadowsocks is more robust.

Shadowsocks

Shadowsocks is not technically a VPN protocol — it is a secure proxy protocol originally designed specifically to circumvent China's Great Firewall. Its core innovation is traffic obfuscation: Shadowsocks traffic is designed to be indistinguishable from ordinary HTTPS traffic, making it extraordinarily resistant to DPI-based blocking. While WireGuard and OpenVPN produce identifiable VPN traffic signatures that censors can detect and block, Shadowsocks produces traffic that looks like web browsing.

Shadowsocks supports strong encryption: AES-256-GCM and ChaCha20-Poly1305 are both supported ciphers. Used with a secure cipher, it provides equivalent data security to a traditional VPN protocol while adding a layer of obfuscation that traditional VPNs cannot match. Vizoguard uses Shadowsocks as its underlying transport for this reason — it delivers both encryption and censorship resistance in markets where standard VPN protocols are actively blocked.

IKEv2/IPSec

IKEv2 (Internet Key Exchange version 2) paired with IPSec is a mature protocol with strong support on mobile platforms, particularly iOS and macOS where it is implemented natively. It handles network changes well — useful when switching between Wi-Fi and cellular — and supports a range of ciphers including AES-256. IKEv2 has been audited and is considered secure when correctly configured.

Its limitations: IKEv2 traffic is identifiable and can be blocked on restrictive networks; it is also proprietary in some implementations (Microsoft and Cisco have their own variants). For users who need censorship resistance, IKEv2 is not the right choice. For corporate or mobile users on open networks who want a stable, fast connection, it remains a solid option.

Kill Switch, DNS Leak Protection, and Split Tunneling

Strong encryption and a solid protocol are necessary but not sufficient for a truly secure VPN. Operational security features determine whether your real identity is protected even when things go wrong.

Kill Switch: The Last Line of Defense

A kill switch (also called a network lock) cuts your internet access if the VPN tunnel drops unexpectedly. VPN connections drop for many reasons: server maintenance, network congestion, switching between Wi-Fi and cellular, or a crash in the VPN client. Without a kill switch, these drops expose your real IP address and unencrypted traffic to your ISP and any network observer — even for the few seconds before you notice the disconnection and reconnect manually.

A properly implemented kill switch operates at the network driver or firewall level, not the application level. Application-level kill switches — which tell the VPN app to block traffic when the tunnel drops — have a race condition: there is a window between the tunnel dropping and the application layer reacting. A kernel-level or firewall-level kill switch (using iptables or Windows Filtering Platform) blocks traffic before any data can leave the device.

The kill switch should also be enabled by default. A feature buried in advanced settings that users must discover and enable is not meaningfully providing security — most users will never find it.

DNS Leak Prevention: Closing the Plaintext Backdoor

DNS (Domain Name System) queries are the requests your device sends to resolve domain names (like google.com) into IP addresses. By default, your operating system sends these queries to your ISP's resolver — in plaintext, unencrypted. Even with a VPN active, some implementations fail to route DNS through the encrypted tunnel, creating a "DNS leak" where your ISP can see every domain you visit regardless of the VPN.

DNS leak prevention routes all DNS queries through the VPN's own encrypted resolvers. A complete implementation also applies DNS over TLS (DoT) or DNS over HTTPS (DoH) within the tunnel for an additional layer of encryption. You can verify DNS leak protection at any time by visiting dnsleaktest.com while connected to the VPN — all DNS servers listed should belong to your VPN provider, not your ISP.

WebRTC leaks are a related threat: modern browsers use the WebRTC API for peer-to-peer communication, and this API can expose your real local IP address even through a VPN. Secure VPN clients disable WebRTC at the browser level or use firewall rules to block WebRTC traffic outside the tunnel. Browser extensions like uBlock Origin can also prevent WebRTC leaks.

Split Tunneling: Controlled Flexibility

Split tunneling lets you designate specific apps or destination IP ranges to route through the VPN while other traffic connects directly. A typical use case: routing your browser and email client through the VPN for privacy while letting a video conferencing app connect directly to reduce latency.

From a security perspective, split tunneling is a deliberate trade-off. Traffic routed outside the tunnel is unprotected — your ISP can see it, and it reveals your real IP to the destination. For users who understand which traffic they need protected and which they do not, split tunneling is a useful optimization. For users who are uncertain, full-tunnel mode (all traffic through the VPN) is the safer default. A secure VPN should offer split tunneling as an option, not as a default setting.

Logging Policies: What to Watch For

Encryption protects your data while it travels across the network. A no-log policy protects your data from the VPN provider itself — and from anyone who can legally compel the provider to hand over data.

What "No Logs" Should Mean

A genuine no-log (or zero-log) policy means the provider does not collect or retain:

  • Your browsing history or DNS queries
  • Your real IP address assigned to a session
  • Timestamps of when you connected or disconnected
  • The amount of data transferred per session
  • The VPN server or exit node used

Some providers claim "no logs" while retaining connection metadata — timestamps, session durations, bandwidth used — which can still be used to correlate activity to individuals. Read the actual privacy policy, not the marketing summary. Look for specific language about what is and is not stored, under what circumstances data may be shared with third parties, and what happens in response to law enforcement requests.

Verified vs. Self-Declared

Self-declared no-log policies are marketing claims. A credible no-log policy is verified in one of two ways:

  • Independent audit: A recognized security firm (Cure53, SEC Consult, Deloitte, PwC) examines the provider's infrastructure and confirms that logging does not occur. The audit report should be publicly available. Audits should be repeated periodically — a 2019 audit does not verify 2026 operations.
  • Legal precedent: The provider has been subject to a court order, government seizure, or law enforcement request and demonstrably had no logs to produce. This is the most definitive verification possible — the legal system has tested the policy and found it genuine.

Jurisdiction Matters

Where a VPN company is incorporated determines which legal systems can compel data disclosure. VPN providers based in Fourteen Eyes countries (US, UK, Canada, Australia, New Zealand, and allied nations) are subject to intelligence-sharing agreements and national security letters that may not notify the target or allow legal challenge. Providers in jurisdictions outside these alliances — Malta (EU), British Virgin Islands, Switzerland, Panama, Romania — face higher bars for compelled disclosure and no mandatory participation in mass surveillance programs.

How Hackers Exploit Insecure VPNs

Understanding attack vectors helps clarify why each security layer matters. These are documented, real-world techniques used against insecure VPN implementations:

  • 1
    VPN Downgrade Attacks Some VPN clients attempt to negotiate a connection using the strongest available protocol and silently fall back to weaker ones if the server is unresponsive or the network blocks certain ports. An attacker who controls the network can manipulate this negotiation to force the client into using a weaker cipher or deprecated protocol. Secure VPN clients disallow fallback to insecure protocols — if WireGuard is selected, it connects or fails, never silently downgrades to PPTP.
  • 2
    Traffic Correlation Attacks Even with encryption, an attacker who can observe both your outgoing connection (to the VPN server) and the VPN server's outgoing traffic (to the destination) can correlate packet timing and volume to identify which sites you are visiting — even without decrypting anything. This attack requires nation-state-level resources and access to multiple network observation points. For most users, it is not a practical threat, but VPNs that use traffic padding and timing randomization provide better resistance.
  • 3
    DNS Hijacking Through Insecure Resolvers If a VPN leaks DNS outside the tunnel, or uses an unencrypted resolver, an attacker on the local network can respond to DNS queries with falsified answers — redirecting you to a malicious server while the address bar shows the legitimate domain name. This is the foundation of many phishing attacks on public Wi-Fi. DNS over TLS within the VPN tunnel prevents this attack entirely.
  • 4
    Fake VPN Apps and Man-in-the-Middle Proxies Malicious VPN apps — particularly free VPNs distributed outside official app stores — sometimes function as man-in-the-middle proxies that intercept and inspect traffic rather than encrypting it. The app shows a "connected" UI state while routing traffic through an attacker-controlled server. A 2020 study found over 38% of free VPN apps contained malware or malicious code. Only install VPN apps from official app stores and verified providers. See our guide to what malware is and how it spreads.
  • 5
    Session Hijacking After VPN Drop Without a kill switch, a momentary VPN disconnection exposes your real IP. If you are logged into a site that uses IP-based session validation (less common now but still used by some services), this IP change can either reveal your real location or allow an attacker who has pre-positioned themselves on your network to inject traffic into an active session during the exposure window. The kill switch eliminates this window entirely.
  • 6
    Expired Certificate and HSTS Stripping Some insecure VPN implementations perform their own SSL inspection, which requires installing a custom root certificate. An attacker who compromises this certificate chain — or a poorly implemented VPN that fails to validate server certificates correctly — can present valid-looking HTTPS connections to sites that are actually MITM intercepted. Never install a VPN that asks you to trust a custom root CA certificate unless you fully understand the implications.
